Scientists find over 70 chemicals, ARVs in Lake Victoria, caution residents against domestic use of

In a recent article, TUKO.co.ke highlighted how inhabitants of areas along Lake Victoria shores in Homa Bay county bathe, wash and fetch water for domestic use from the same point.

The story attracted the interest of many readers, and while some liked or laughed at the culture, public health experts observed the human activities could expose people to diseases such as diarrhoea, cholera, dysentery, typhoid, polio bilharzia among others.

TUKO.co.ke reached out to Faith Kandie a researcher on water pollution to get her expert opinion on the risk of these human activities on the residents’ health.

Kandie explained that a recent study in collaboration with scientists from the International Centre of Insect Physiology and Ecology (ICIPE), Kenya Medical Research Institute (KEMRI) and Germany (Helmholtz Center for Environmental Research-UFZ, Goethe University - Frankfurt and RWTH Aachen) within Lake Victoria South basin showed a total of 78 chemicals were found in water, 30 in snails and 78 in sediments.

The study was carried out in seven counties of Homa Bay, Migori, Kisii, Nyamira, Narok, Kisumu and Kericho and the water, snails and sediments were the samples used.

Kandie noted that the samples were from rivers, drainage canals, reservoirs and rice irrigation fields from the basin.

"A total of forty-eight sites were sampled. Chemical analysis was performed using advanced instrument technology based at the Helmholtz Center for Environmental Research-UFZ in Leipzig," she said.

She revealed that most of the chemicals found were pesticides and biocides adding that some of those found in part of the water included a metabolite of the antibiotic sulfamethoxazole, the food additive triethyl citrate, the anti-retroviral (ARV) drug nevirapine and the herbicide hexazinone.

Kandie said, in snails, the pesticide atrazine was found in high concentrations (up to 375 ng/g wet weight) while the pesticide pirimiphos-methyl also known as actellic and efavirenz were found in sediments.

"The high pesticide concentrations could be attributed to the local land use especially those found in the large agro-industrial sugarcane and rice plantations, and those used in mosquito control," the expert said."Due to surface runoff, pesticides are washed into surface water bodies which are typically not protected by riparian strips." the expert added

She also noted that the presence of HIV/AIDS therapy drugs in the environment could be linked to the high prevalence of HIV reported in the western region.

The researcher said that within the study, they also evaluated the risk to public health by investigating the contribution of pesticides found in water to the transmission of bilharzia (also known as schistosomiasis), a disease prevalent in the area.

The results showed that the snails responsible for bilharzia were more tolerant when exposed to selected pesticides compared to sensitive macroinvertebrates.

"The results showed that pesticide pollution is a major driver to the increased abundance of the host snails which results in a higher risk of bilharzia transmission along the region," she said.

According to Kandie, the risk assessment from chemicals found in water and sediment revealed acute effects to crustaceans (shellfishes) while fish and algae experienced majorly chronic risk.

At the same time, she urged the national and county governments to come up with policies on regulating the use of agricultural pesticides.

"Let the government come up with policies that will regulate the use of the pesticides and their disposal when expired," the expert said.

She also urged the government to come up with infrastructural developments for the provision of safe, clean and accessible water to the community so as to avoid contact and consumption of polluted water from the water bodies.

"The water, especially from the studied water bodies, is not safe for domestic use. The government can also come up with ways of providing clean water for the people within the lake basin to curb the spread water-borne diseases especially bilharzia," the expert said.

The study, therefore, highlights the reason for residents not to be comfortable using Lake Victoria water for bathing, drinking, cleaning and general domestic use.
