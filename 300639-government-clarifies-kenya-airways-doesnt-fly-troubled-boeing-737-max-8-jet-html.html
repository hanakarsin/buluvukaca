Government clarifies Kenya Airways doesn't fly troubled Boeing 737 Max 8 jet

- Several countries have grounded Boeing 737 Max jets following Ethiopian Airlines plane crash

- The Sunday, March 10, accident claimed lives of 157 people, among them 32 Kenyans

- CS Macharia assured families affected by the plane crash of full support from the national government

A section of Kenyans have been wondering why everyone else seems to be grounding Boeing 737 Max 8 jets following the fatal Ethiopian Airlines plane crash except Kenya.

Well, it is because Kenya's flag carrier, Kenya Airways (KQ), which has been flying on huge losses and debts for close to eight consecutive years, does not have that kind of aircraft type in its fleet.

In a statement issued on Wednesday, March 13, Transport CS James Macharia confirmed there was no single airline in the country that operates Boeing 737 Max 8 jet being grounded by other countries.

“We wish to state that no Kenya air operator has in their fleet the affected aircraft type,” he noted even as he assured families of those who perished in the Ethiopian Airlines' plane crash of government's full support.

READ ALSO: Ethiopian Airlines: Africa’s largest flag carrier flies high with zero funding from government

TUKO.co.ke understands Kenya Airways currently has a fleet size of about 44 aircraft including all-Boeing long-haul fleet of one 777-300ER, six 767-300ERs and four 777-200ERs.

The Kenyan national carrier has been working closely with its Ethiopian counterpart to support victims of the Sunday, March 10, accident which left a total of 157 people dead.

Kenya was the hardest hit by the tragedy as it lost 32 of its citizens in the crash.

Besides the 32 Kenyan victims, a total of 18 Canadians, six Egyptians, nine Ethiopians, seven French, eight Americans and seven Britons among other nationalities also died in the accident.

The tragedy prompted several countries across the world to ban Boeing 737 Max 8 jets from flying in their airspaces with the latest being the United States.

Investigators are ongoing and so far the flight recorders from the Ethiopian Airlines' plane have been recovered and being examined in efforts to establish what caused the crash.
